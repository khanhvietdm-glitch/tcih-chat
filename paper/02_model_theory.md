# 3. The TCIH Model

This section develops the corrected formal model. The guiding decision is to treat a vertex as a **labelled judgment** and a proof as an **ordered event structure**, so that assumption identity, scope, and discharge are first-class and survive the sharing (folding) operation.

## 3.1. Preliminaries and labelled contexts

Fix a signature $\Sigma=(\mathrm{Sorts},\mathrm{Funct},\mathrm{Pred})$ in the usual sense of many-sorted logic. The formal results and the implementation concern the propositional fragment $\mathrm{Form}_\Sigma$ generated by a set of atoms together with $\bot,\ \neg,\ \wedge,\ \vee,\ \Rightarrow$; first-order quantifiers and equality enter only through the semantic oracle (Section 5) and are discussed in Section 9. A substitution $\sigma$ is a finite map from schema atoms to formulas, with the standard capture-free action written $\varphi[\sigma]$. We write $|\varphi|$ for the number of nodes in the syntax tree of $\varphi$.

The central data structure is the **labelled context**.

> **Definition 3.1 (labelled context).** Let $\mathrm{AID}$ be a countable set of *assumption identifiers*. A labelled context is a finite partial map
> $$\Gamma : \mathrm{AID} \rightharpoonup \mathrm{Form}_\Sigma,$$
> presented as a finite set of bindings $\{a_1{:}\psi_1,\dots,a_k{:}\psi_k\}$ with distinct identifiers $a_i$. We write $\mathrm{dom}(\Gamma)$ for $\{a_1,\dots,a_k\}$ and $\Gamma(a)$ for the formula bound to $a$.

Two operations on labelled contexts drive the activation condition below.

> **Definition 3.2 (consistent union, restriction).** The *consistent union* $\biguplus_i \Gamma_i$ is defined when no identifier is bound to two different formulas across the $\Gamma_i$, in which case it is their set-union; otherwise it is undefined. The *restriction* $\Gamma \!\restriction\! (\mathrm{dom}(\Gamma)\setminus D)$ removes the bindings whose identifiers lie in $D$.

Modelling the context as a map keyed by identifiers — rather than as a set of formula contents — is the correction that lets two hypotheses of identical content $A$ be kept distinct: they receive distinct identifiers $a\neq a'$, so $\{a{:}A\}$ and $\{a'{:}A\}$ are different contexts. (This is the executable content of the regression test `model`.)

## 3.2. Rule schemas and instances

> **Definition 3.3 (rule schema).** A rule schema is a tuple
> $$R=\bigl(\mathrm{Prem}(R),\ \mathrm{Conc}(R),\ \mathrm{Disch}(R),\ \mathrm{Sort}(R)\bigr),$$
> where $\mathrm{Prem}(R)$ is a finite multiset of premise formula-schemas, $\mathrm{Conc}(R)$ is a single conclusion formula-schema, $\mathrm{Disch}(R)$ is a finite multiset of *dischargeable formula-schemas*, and $\mathrm{Sort}(R)$ is the sort on which the schema acts.

The decisive change from the precursor is that $\mathrm{Disch}(R)$ is **not** a subset of $\mathrm{Prem}(R)$. A discharged hypothesis is not a peer premise of the rule; it is an assumption living in the *context* of a premise's derivation. For implication-introduction the premise read off the source judgment $\Gamma,a{:}A \vdash B$ is $B$, while the discharged object is the assumption carrying $A$:
$$\mathrm{Prem}(\Rightarrow\!I)=[\,B\,],\quad \mathrm{Conc}(\Rightarrow\!I)=A\Rightarrow B,\quad \mathrm{Disch}(\Rightarrow\!I)=[\,A\,].$$
Table 2 lists the schemas implemented; $\bot$-rules and the classical $\mathrm{RAA}$ give the negation fragment.

| Rule | $\mathrm{Prem}$ | $\mathrm{Conc}$ | $\mathrm{Disch}$ |
|------|-----------------|-----------------|------------------|
| $\Rightarrow\!I$ | $B$ | $A\Rightarrow B$ | $A$ |
| $\Rightarrow\!E$ (MP) | $A\Rightarrow B,\ A$ | $B$ | — |
| $\wedge I$ | $A,\ B$ | $A\wedge B$ | — |
| $\wedge E_{1,2}$ | $A\wedge B$ | $A$ / $B$ | — |
| $\vee I_{1,2}$ | $A$ / $B$ | $A\vee B$ | — |
| $\vee E$ | $A\vee B,\ C,\ C$ | $C$ | $A,\ B$ |
| $\neg I$ | $\bot$ | $\neg A$ | $A$ |
| $\neg E$ | $A,\ \neg A$ | $\bot$ | — |
| $\bot E$ | $\bot$ | $C$ | — |
| $\mathrm{RAA}$ (classical) | $\bot$ | $A$ | $\neg A$ |

**Table 2.** The natural-deduction rule library. Leaf events `Assume` and `Axiom` (no premises) open a hypothesis $a{:}A\vdash A$ and an axiom $\vdash A$ respectively.

> **Definition 3.4 (rule instance).** A rule instance $R[\sigma]$ applies a substitution $\sigma$ defined on the schema atoms; its premises, conclusion and dischargeable formulas are $\mathrm{Prem}(R)[\sigma]$, $\mathrm{Conc}(R)[\sigma]$, $\mathrm{Disch}(R)[\sigma]$.

## 3.3. Vertices, events, and the hypergraph

> **Definition 3.5 (vertex).** A vertex is a labelled judgment
> $$v=\bigl(\mathrm{id}_v,\ \Gamma_v \vdash \varphi_v,\ \tau_v\bigr),$$
> with a unique identifier $\mathrm{id}_v$, a labelled context $\Gamma_v$, a content $\varphi_v$, and a sort $\tau_v$. The **label** of $v$ is the triple $(\Gamma_v,\varphi_v,\tau_v)$; the identifier is not part of the label.

> **Definition 3.6 (event / hyperedge).** An event is
> $$e=\bigl(S_e,\ t_e,\ R[\sigma_e],\ D_e,\ \mathrm{prov}_e\bigr),$$
> where $S_e$ is a finite multiset of source vertices (incidences), $t_e$ is the target vertex, $R[\sigma_e]$ a rule instance, $D_e\subseteq\mathrm{AID}$ the set of assumption identifiers discharged *at this event*, and $\mathrm{prov}_e$ an optional provenance (expansion-block) tag.

> **Definition 3.7 (well-formed event).** An event $e$ is structurally well-formed when
> 1. (premises) the multiset of source contents equals $\mathrm{Prem}(R[\sigma_e])$;
> 2. (conclusion) $\varphi_{t_e}=\mathrm{Conc}(R[\sigma_e])$;
> 3. (sorts) the source and target sorts are compatible with $\mathrm{Sort}(R)$;
> 4. (**activation**) $\;\Gamma_{t_e}=\bigl(\biguplus_{s\in S_e}\Gamma_s\bigr)\restriction\bigl(\mathrm{dom}\setminus D_e\bigr)$, the consistent union being defined;
> 5. (**discharge coherence**) every $a\in D_e$ is bound in some source context, with $\bigl(\biguplus_{s}\Gamma_s\bigr)(a)\in\mathrm{Disch}(R[\sigma_e])$, and $a\notin\mathrm{dom}(\Gamma_{t_e})$.

Condition 4 *computes* the target context from the sources by union and removal of the discharged identifiers; condition 5 says discharge removes exactly named, genuinely-open assumptions carrying the formula the rule is entitled to discharge. The number of discharged identifiers may be zero (*vacuous* discharge, e.g. $B$ in $A\Rightarrow(B\Rightarrow A)$) or greater than one (*multiple* discharge), exactly as in standard natural deduction; the rule fixes *which* formula may be discharged, via $\sigma$ on the antecedent, not *how many* occurrences. (This is what the test `a_imp_a` together with the K-combinator case in the prover benchmark exercises.)

> **Definition 3.8 (TCIH and the event order).** A TCIH over $(\Sigma,\mathcal R)$ is a tuple $G=(V,E,\lambda_V,\lambda_E,\prec)$ where $\lambda_V,\lambda_E$ label vertices and events as above, every event is well-formed, and $\prec$ is the *event-dependency order*: $e'\prec e$ iff $t_{e'}\in S_e$. We require every vertex to be the target of exactly one event (its **unique justification**); leaves are targets of `Assume`/`Axiom` events.

The open/discharged status of an assumption is therefore **derived**, not stored: identifier $a$ is *open at $v$* iff $a\in\mathrm{dom}(\Gamma_v)$, and is *discharged by $e$* iff $a\in D_e$. There is no mutable flag, which removes the temporal inconsistency of the precursor. Acyclicity is a property of $\prec$.

> **Proposition 3.9 (acyclicity from order).** The relation on vertices induced by $\prec$ (an edge from each $s\in S_e$ to $t_e$) is acyclic; equivalently, the map sending a vertex to the length of the longest event-chain ending at it is a strictly increasing rank.

*Proof.* The unique-justification requirement makes the source-to-target relation a function-graph layered by event order; a rank function exists by the standard topological argument, and a graph admitting a strictly increasing rank has no directed cycle. $\square$

> **Definition 3.10 (semantic validity).** A well-formed event is *semantically valid* when $\bigl\{\varphi_s : s\in S_e\bigr\}\models \varphi_{t_e}$ in the background logic (for discharging rules, the validity is that of the corresponding entailment with the discharged assumptions moved across the turnstile). A TCIH is a *derivation* when every event is both well-formed and semantically valid.

This is the split the whole paper turns on: well-formedness (Definition 3.7) is combinatorial and cheap; semantic validity (Definition 3.10) is logic-dependent. Section 5 makes the cost precise.

## 3.4. The monotone fragment, closure, and proof length

For the **discharge-free, monotone (Horn)** fragment the classical closure picture is exact, and it is *only* there that it is exact — a point the precursor over-extended.

> **Definition 3.11 (Horn closure).** For a definite-Horn library $\mathcal R$ (atomic conclusions, no discharge, no disjunction) and a set $H$ of atomic assumptions, the one-step operator $T_\mathcal{R}(X)=X\cup\{\mathrm{Conc}(r[\sigma]) : \mathrm{Prem}(r[\sigma])\subseteq X\}$ has least fixed point $\mathrm{Cl}_\mathcal R(H)$.

> **Proposition 3.12 (length = shortest path, Horn only).** In the Horn fragment, the minimal number of events in a TCIH-derivation of an atom $\varphi$ from $H$ equals the length of a shortest path from $H$ to a state containing $\varphi$ in the state-transition graph on subsets of atoms. Outside the Horn fragment this identification fails: a faithful state must record open scopes and fresh-variable status, not merely a set of available formulas, because discharge changes the context.

The implementation realizes the Horn case by the linear-time forward-chaining algorithm of Section 5; the cautionary half of Proposition 3.12 is why the automated prover for the full propositional fragment (Section 6) is a *proof-constructing search*, not a closure computation.

# 4. Theoretical Results

We state and prove the four results that make the TCIH-Chat pipeline sound. Proof sketches are given here; the longer arguments appear in Appendix A. Each theorem is accompanied by the regression test that exercises it.

## 4.1. Soundness and syntactic relative completeness

> **Theorem 4.1 (soundness).** Let $G$ be a TCIH-derivation (every event well-formed and semantically valid). Then for every vertex $v$, $\ \{\varphi_s : s\ \text{a leaf below}\ v\} \models \varphi_v$, and in particular the open context $\Gamma_v$ entails $\varphi_v$.

*Proof.* Induction along the event order $\prec$ (which is well-founded by Proposition 3.9). Leaves hold by definition; at an internal event, the induction hypothesis gives entailment of the source contents from the leaves below, and semantic validity propagates the entailment to the target, with discharge correctly moving the named assumptions across the turnstile by condition 5 of Definition 3.7. $\square$

> **Theorem 4.2 (representability of discharge).** Every theorem of the propositional fragment derivable in natural deduction is the content of the target of some TCIH-derivation; in particular $\vdash A\Rightarrow A$ is derivable (open $a{:}A\vdash A$ by `Assume`, then $\Rightarrow\!I$ discharging $a$).

The precursor could not express this two-step derivation because it had no assumption-opening event; here it is the smallest gallery item (`a_imp_a`, $|V|=|E|=2$). Completeness *relative* to the background logic is inherited from natural deduction; semantic completeness is a property of the chosen oracle, not of the framework.

## 4.2. Folding: sharing repeated subproofs, soundly

Folding shares vertices to compress a derivation. The question is *which* identifications are sound.

> **Definition 4.3 (folding).** A folding of $G$ is a surjection $f:V(G)\to V(G')$ that respects contents and rule labels and induces a map on events. It is **label-preserving** (equivalently *order- and context-preserving*) if it identifies vertices only when their full labels $(\Gamma,\varphi,\tau)$ coincide, and it is **content-only** if it identifies on $\varphi$ alone.

> **Theorem 4.4 (Order- and Context-Preserving Folding).**
> (a) *Sufficiency.* If $f$ is label-preserving and the quotient is acyclic, then $f(G)$ is again a TCIH-derivation: well-formedness conditions 1–5 and semantic validity are preserved, source-incidence multiplicities and discharge sets $D_e$ are preserved, and unique justification is preserved (structurally identical justifications are merged).
> (b) *Necessity.* There is a derivation $G$ and a content-only folding $f$ such that $f(G)$ violates the discharge/activation conditions and is rejected by the verifier.

*Proof.* (a) Each condition of Definition 3.7 is stated in terms of contents, sorts, labelled contexts and discharge sets, all of which $f$ preserves pointwise; the consistent union in condition 4 remains defined because identified vertices have identical contexts. Semantic validity depends only on the rule instance, which is unchanged. Acyclicity is assumed (and certified below). (b) Take $G$ to be the valid derivation of $(A\Rightarrow A)\wedge A$ from $\{A\}$, which contains an inner hypothesis $a_{\mathrm{in}}{:}A\vdash A$ (later discharged by $\Rightarrow\!I$) and an outer assumption $a_{\mathrm{out}}{:}A\vdash A$ (which stays open). Their labels differ ($a_\mathrm{in}\neq a_\mathrm{out}$ in the context), so a label-preserving fold leaves them apart; a content-only fold merges them, after which the $\Rightarrow\!I$ event would have to discharge an identifier absent from its (now merged) source context and the merged vertex would carry two justifications. The verifier reports exactly this (activation failure and discharge-without-witness). $\square$

This is the executable witness in tests `fold` (content-only fold rejected; label-preserving fold accepted) and is the corrected form of the precursor's folding claim: it now names the *full* label, the multiplicities, the discharge identifiers, and acyclicity, and is renamed accordingly.

The remaining question is when the quotient is acyclic. The precursor asserted (its Proposition 4.6.1) that it suffices for each equivalence class to be a reachability *antichain*. **This is false.**

> **Proposition 4.5 (antichain is insufficient; rank certificate is sufficient).** There is a DAG and an equivalence whose classes are reachability antichains but whose quotient has a directed cycle. Conversely, if there is a rank function $\rho:V\to\mathbb N$ with $u\sim v\Rightarrow\rho(u)=\rho(v)$ and $\rho(u)<\rho(w)$ for every edge $u\to w$, then the quotient is acyclic.

*Proof.* Counterexample: vertices $a_1,a_2,b_1,b_2$ with edges $a_1\to b_1$ and $b_2\to a_2$, classes $A=\{a_1,a_2\}$, $B=\{b_1,b_2\}$. No directed path joins the two members of either class, so each class is an antichain; yet the quotient has $A\to B$ (from $a_1\to b_1$) and $B\to A$ (from $b_2\to a_2$), a $2$-cycle. Sufficiency: a quotient edge $[u]\to[w]$ lifts to some edge $u'\to w'$ with $\rho(u')<\rho(w')$, and $\rho$ is constant on classes, so $\rho$ strictly increases along every quotient edge, precluding a cycle. $\square$

The counterexample and the rank certificate are tests `antichain` and the `rank_certifies_acyclic` check; in the implementation the verifier simply tests the quotient for acyclicity directly (linear time), with the rank function available as the certificate.

## 4.3. Granularity as provenance-marked macro-elimination

The precursor's "Granularity Invariance Theorem" was stated too strongly. We reposition it as what it is — *macro-rule elimination* — and mark expansions with provenance so that expansion and contraction are exact inverses.

Let $r$ be a derived (admissible) macro-rule with a base definition $D_r$ in a sub-library $\mathcal R_0\subseteq\mathcal R\setminus\{r\}$, and let $\mathcal R'=(\mathcal R\setminus\{r\})\cup\mathcal R_0=\mathcal R\setminus\{r\}$. Write $\mathrm{expand}$ for the operation replacing each $r$-event by a fresh copy of $D_r$ whose inserted events carry a provenance tag for that block, and $\mathrm{contract}$ for the reverse on provenance-marked blocks.

> **Theorem 4.6 (granularity).** For every $H,\varphi$:
> (i) **Derivability** is preserved: $H\vdash_{\mathcal R}\varphi$ iff $H\vdash_{\mathcal R'}\varphi$.
> (ii) **Inverse on marked derivations.** On the class of $\mathcal R'$-derivations whose $\mathcal R_0$-steps occur exactly inside provenance-marked $D_r$-blocks, $\mathrm{expand}$ and $\mathrm{contract}$ are mutually inverse up to isomorphism.
> (iii) **Length law.** $\ |\mathrm{expand}(G)| = |G| + k_r(G)\,(|D_r|-1)$, where $k_r(G)$ is the number of $r$-events in $G$; hence $\ \ell_{\mathcal R}(H,\varphi)\le \ell_{\mathcal R'}(H,\varphi)\le \min_{G}\bigl[\,|G|+k_r(G)(|D_r|-1)\,\bigr]$ over length-minimal $\mathcal R$-derivations, a **constructive** upper bound; it is attained on the family of Section 8.
> (iv) **Corrected invariant.** The multiset of base rules occurring *outside* the marked blocks is preserved by contraction.

*Proof sketch.* (i) Each direction substitutes a block for a macro-event or conversely, preserving well-formedness (the block's external sources and target coincide with the macro's) and semantic validity ($D_r$ derives $\mathrm{Conc}(r)$ from $\mathrm{Prem}(r)$). (ii) Provenance tags identify exactly the inserted blocks, so contraction has no pattern-overlap ambiguity. (iii) Each expansion removes one event and inserts $|D_r|$, the last of which reuses the macro's target; summing over the $k_r(G)$ macro-events gives the law, and the upper bound follows by expanding a length-minimal $\mathcal R$-derivation. (iv) Outside the marked blocks the two derivations have identical events. $\square$

Part (iv) is the correction of the precursor's Corollary 4.12(b), which wrongly claimed the *whole* rule multiset is preserved (expansion adds the $\mathcal R_0$-rules of the block). The length law, round-trip identity, and invariant are verified for $k\in\{1,2,3,5,8,13\}$ in test `granularity` and reported in Section 8; the bound is attained on the HS-chain family there.

## 4.4. Cut-elimination as semantic confluence

For the simply-typed implicational–conjunctive fragment, cut steps are local graph rewrites. We encode TCIH-derivations as typed $\lambda$-terms, $\llbracket\cdot\rrbracket$, mapping $\Rightarrow\!I$/$\Rightarrow\!E$ to abstraction/application and $\wedge I$/$\wedge E$ to pairing/projection.

> **Theorem 4.7 (normalization and semantic confluence).** On this fragment the cut-rewrite system is (a) **sound** — each step maps a derivation to a derivation with the same end-judgment; (b) **strongly normalizing** — every rewrite step $\beta$-reduces the encoding, so an infinite rewrite chain would contradict strong normalization of the simply-typed $\lambda$-calculus; and (c) **semantically confluent after encoding**: any two normal forms of a derivation have $\alpha$-equivalent encodings after sharing erasure.

*Proof sketch.* (a)/(b) are the forward-simulation: a graph rewrite $G\rightsquigarrow G'$ satisfies $\llbracket G\rrbracket\to_\beta\llbracket G'\rrbracket$, and Theorem 4.1 preserves the end-judgment. (c) follows from Church–Rosser for the simply-typed $\lambda$-calculus applied to the encodings, then transported back. $\square$

We deliberately claim only confluence **after encoding and sharing erasure**, not graph-level confluence. The precursor asserted that $\alpha$-equivalent encodings imply graphs differing only by a folding; that converse is not established (the encoding need not be injective modulo folding) and is not used here. This is the honest downgrade requested in review; the stronger statement (full abstraction or injectivity of the encoding modulo a precisely defined equivalence) is left open (Section 9).
