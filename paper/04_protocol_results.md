# 7. Datasets and Experimental Protocol

We separate what is **measured in this paper** from what the protocol is **designed to measure** once an annotated logical human/LLM corpus exists. Every number in Section 8 is measured and regenerated deterministically by the released scripts; where an evaluation is out of current scope (semantic E4 and naturally occurring errors), we say so explicitly rather than estimating numbers.

## 7.1. Datasets

- **D1 — Corrected case studies (gallery).** Seven hand-built derivations exercising the model: $A\Rightarrow A$, modus ponens, hypothetical syllogism, modus tollens, both directions of the intuitionistic De Morgan equivalence $\neg(A\vee B)\dashv\vdash(\neg A\wedge\neg B)$, and the $(A\Rightarrow A)\wedge A$ folding witness. Each is checked for well-formedness.
- **D2 — Construction benchmark.** Twenty-five propositional theorems (nineteen intuitionistic, six classical), including the K and S combinators, distribution laws, the De Morgan family, and inferences from assumptions (modus tollens, disjunctive syllogism, case analysis). For each, the engine must construct a derivation that independently passes structural verification *and* the semantic oracle.
- **D3 — Synthetic ground-truth error corpus.** From each correct benchmark proof we generate one variant per structural/semantic class by the injection harness: E1 (drop a premise), E2 (corrupt a target context), E3 (discharge a non-open identifier), E4 (replace a sound step by a formal fallacy, e.g. affirming the consequent). Each variant carries a ground-truth label *(class, locus)*. This corpus yields **real** detection and localization measurements because the labels are known by construction.
- **D4 — Real proof-graph corpus.** A pipeline (`proof_graph_parser.py`) ingests $211{,}922$ step-structured solutions across algebra, number theory, geometry, calculus, and probability/combinatorics into two-level proof graphs, giving the structural statistics that motivate the design (node counts, arities).
- **D5 — Upgraded-parser corpus and the annotated extension.** The rule-classifying parser (`nl_parser`) realizes the D4→D5 upgrade: it re-derives faithful structure and a rule classification for the real corpus, on which §8.9 reports a measured error-detection evaluation (real proof structures, injected ground-truth faults). The *fully annotated* human/LLM corpus — proofs in a logical fragment labelled with E1–E6, error locus, scope, and ground-truth validity, for evaluating *semantic* and *naturally occurring* errors — remains the principal outstanding data task (§9).

## 7.2. Baselines

- **B1 — Formula-only proof DAG**: vertices carry contents only (no labelled context, no discharge identifiers) — the classical proof-DAG view.
- **B2 — Checker without $\Gamma$**: activation condition (4) disabled.
- **B3 — Checker without discharge identifiers**: condition (5) disabled.
- **B4 — Proof-assistant validation**: whole-proof accept/reject by an external prover, with no structural localization (the status quo for LLM-proof evaluation).

B1–B3 are *ablations* of TCIH-Chat and isolate the contribution of each new component; B4 is the external point of comparison that returns a single bit instead of a localized class.

## 7.3. Metrics

Precision, recall and $F_1$ per error class; macro-averaged $F_1$; **false-rejection rate** (fraction of *correct* proofs wrongly flagged); **localization accuracy** (fraction of detected errors whose reported locus equals the ground-truth first-error event); parser extraction accuracy (for D5); and runtime/memory versus $N$.

## 7.4. Ablations and robustness

Ablations remove, in turn, the labelled context, the assumption identifiers, the sort, the substitution check, and the event order, measuring the drop in detection. Robustness scenarios stress nested assumptions, repeated identical formulas, shared subproofs, and malformed cycles — the cases on which the formula-only view is known to fail (Theorem 4.4(b)).

## 8.9. Upgraded parser and structural error detection on the real corpus (measured)

To move beyond the legacy parser's heuristic structure (§8.8) we built a rule-classifying parser (`tcih/nl_parser.py`) and evaluated it on **30,000** real solution proofs. It (i) classifies each step into a computational inference taxonomy from textual cues, and (ii) re-infers each step's premises from the values it actually uses — depending on earlier steps whose *result* it consumes, and otherwise on the *problem hypothesis*, **never on the merely-previous step**.

The effect on edge structure is decisive (Table 12). The legacy parser's **57.7%** sequential-fallback edges — which spuriously chained independent parallel computations — are **eliminated (0.0%)** and replaced by faithful hypothesis-attachment (**64.0%**) and value-dependency (**21.7%**) edges. Step classification assigns a named inference type to **88%** of steps (rule-class distribution: Compute 36.8%, Conclude 24.6%, Solve 9.2%, Define 9.1%, ApplyFact 7.0%, Compare 1.1%, Convert 0.3%, Generic 12.0%). Every one of the 30,000 reconstructed proofs is **well-formed and acyclic** under the corrected `StructuralCheck`, which runs on them directly via the generic `Step` rule.

|---|---:|---:|
| Edges: sequential-fallback | 57.7% | **0.0%** |
| Edges: hypothesis-attached | n/a (chained) | 64.0% |
| Edges: value-dependency | 29.4% | 21.7% |
| Edges: goal | 12.9% | 14.3% |
| Multi-source steps | ~7% | 8.4% |
| Steps with a named rule class | 0% (wildcard) | 88% |

**Table 12.** Upgraded vs legacy parser on the real corpus (regenerated by `run_corpus_eval.py`). The spurious sequential edges are removed; structure becomes hypothesis-rooted and value-dependent.

On this faithful structure we ran a real error-detection experiment: into each of **4,000** real proofs we injected one structural fault of known class and locus — a cycle, a dangling reference, a dropped justification, or a removed value-dependency the step's text still uses (an E1-style missing premise) — and measured detection by the corrected checker plus the parser's dependency-consistency check. The result (Table 13) is **recall 1.0 on all four classes**, **exact localization**, and a **0.0 false-rejection rate** on the 4,000 clean proofs. Unlike the synthetic gallery of §8.5, these are *real proof structures* from the corpus, with ground-truth labels supplied by the controlled injection.

**What this does and does not establish.** It establishes, *on real data and at scale*, that the upgraded parser produces faithful (no-false-sequential) structure and that the corrected checker detects and localizes structural faults with no false rejections. It does **not** establish detection of *semantic* errors (E4) — the corpus is computational and lacks a domain oracle — nor of *naturally occurring* human/LLM mistakes, which would require an annotated natural-deduction corpus. That natural-error evaluation is the remaining D5 task (§9); the illustrative projection of the previous draft is retired in favour of these measured results. The *semantic* layer (E4) — out of reach on the legacy structure — is taken up next.

## 8.10. Semantic checking on the corpus via the SMT/CAS oracle (measured)

The structural layer (§8.9) leaves *semantic* validity to the oracle. For the computational corpus we exercised the SMT/CAS backend — **Z3** over the rationals with a **SymPy** fallback for radicals — on **29,435** real steps (5,000 proofs), extracting and deciding each step's arithmetic (in)equality. To stay sound the oracle **defers rather than guesses** on claims it cannot decide exactly: those with free variables (they need the proof context), with transcendental/specialized constructs ($\log$, $\sum$, matrices, …), or with the ambiguous percent sign (a $/100$ multiplier vs. a result label).

Coverage and outcomes (Table 14): **20.8%** of steps carry a ground-checkable arithmetic claim, **32.7%** are symbolic (deferred), and **46.5%** carry no formal claim. Of the ground-checkable steps, **98.2%** verify; **0.38%** are flagged as genuine arithmetic errors and **0.10%** as rounding ($=$ written for $\approx$). Manual inspection of the flagged steps confirms they are predominantly genuine slips — e.g. a step asserting $2\cdot 1 + 6\cdot 2 = 16$ (the value is $14$) and $4\cdot\$0.1 = \$40$ (the value is $\$0.40$) — together with $=$-chains that equate unequal expressions; a small residual of LaTeX-parsing false positives cannot be excluded without exhaustive review, which is why the *sound* detection measure below, not the raw flag count, is the headline.

Detection power is measured cleanly by injection: corrupting a verified-true ground equality (RHS $+\,1$) and re-checking yields an E4-detection recall of **0.9998** over 6,003 real equalities — when a step's arithmetic is wrong, the oracle catches it. Together with §8.9 this realizes the full pipeline on real data: the structural checker localizes E1–E3-style faults in linear time, and the SMT/CAS oracle decides the arithmetic E4 obligations the dichotomy (Theorem 5.6) assigns to it.

## 8.11. Extending coverage with contextual SMT (measured)

The ground-checkable fragment of §8.10 excludes steps whose claims contain free variables (e.g. $x=4$, $x+y=-16$, $x>\sqrt 6-\sqrt 2-2$). These become decidable once the **assumptions in scope** are supplied. Using the faithful premise edges of §8.9, for each step we gather the variable-defining equalities of its transitive predecessors and ask Z3 whether the claim is *entailed* — $\text{assumptions}\Rightarrow\text{claim}$ valid, $\text{assumptions}\Rightarrow\neg\text{claim}$ invalid, both satisfiable underdetermined — with square roots handled by auxiliary variables ($s\ge 0,\ s^2=k$), so nonlinear-real claims such as "$x+2>0$ given $x=\sqrt 6-\sqrt 2$" are decided.

Assumptions are gathered from two sources: the relations established by a step's **premises**, and the relations and "positive/negative" constraints parsed from the **problem statement**. On 8,701 real steps (1,500 proofs) this raises the decidable fraction in two stages (Table 15): premise relations lift it from 21.3% to **33.9%**, and adding problem-statement prose lifts it further to **35.6%** (160 additional steps decided), cutting the deferred-symbolic fraction from $\approx$32% to **17.0%**. With context, 1.0% of steps are now flagged invalid (E4) — errors invisible to the ground checker.

## 8.12. Kernel-checked cross-validation with Lean 4 (measured)

The SMT/CAS oracle is fast but trusts Z3 and SymPy. To raise assurance we added a **Lean 4 kernel backend** (`tcih/lean_backend.py`): a ground arithmetic relation is rendered as a proposition over $\mathbb{Q}$ and discharged by Lean's trusted kernel via `by decide` (the negation is tried to separate *refuted* from *unknown*). Only a **core** toolchain is needed — no mathlib — for the decidable fragment.

On a sample of 120 ground arithmetic claims drawn from the corpus, Lean and Z3 **agree on 100%** of the 109 claims that both decide (zero disagreements), and Lean independently flags **27/30** injected corrupted equalities (recall 0.90); the three misses are `decide` timeouts on large rationals, where the SMT backend's decision (recall 0.9998, §8.10) is stronger. Lean is much slower — it spawns the compiler per query ($\approx$5 s/query, $\approx$11 min for the sample) — so it serves as a *high-assurance cross-check on a sample* rather than the primary oracle, exactly the role the dichotomy assigns to a heavyweight back-end. The perfect agreement is independent evidence that the SMT oracle's ground decisions are sound.

Beyond ground arithmetic, we provisioned **Lean + mathlib** (the matching v4.31.0 toolchain and the prebuilt olean cache) to reach genuinely nonlinear-real and $\forall$-quantified obligations that core Lean and the ground oracle cannot express. Through the `lake env` interface, mathlib's `nlinarith`/`norm_num` discharge representative higher-order goals automatically (Table 17): the AM–GM inequality $x^2+y^2\ge 2xy$, the completed square $x^2-2x+1\ge 0$, and the radical inequality $\sqrt 6-\sqrt 2>0$ are all **proved** by Lean's kernel, while the false $x^2\ge 1$ is correctly **refused**. This is the highest-assurance, heaviest tier of the oracle stack (each query loads mathlib, $\approx$30–60 s), so we exercise it on representative obligations rather than the full corpus. Checking *geometry word problems* this way would additionally require autoformalizing prose into mathlib statements — an autoformalization step we do not perform (§9).
